About

The Northeast Physician Scientist Student Symposium is a student-organized event aimed at bringing together MD-PhD and research-track MD students, faculty, as well as undergrads in the Northeast region interested in a physician scientist career. This event is co-organized by students from Boston University, Tufts, University of Massachusetts, and HMS/MIT MD/PhD and MD programs. We are co-sponsored by APSA, the BU School of Medicine (venue and infrastructure support), the Journal of Clinical Investigation, and New England BioLabs.

Registration is open to all interested participants at all institutions regardless of APSA membership. Click HERE to register, or click HERE to submit an abstract. Registration and abstract submission for the conference are FREE for all attendees.

Keynote Speaker: Anthony Monaco, MD, PhD


  Anthony Monaco, MD, PhD is the current president of Tufts University and holds faculty appointments as a professor of biology at the School of Arts and Sciences and professor of neuroscience at Tufts University School of Medicine.
  President Monaco received his bachelorâ€™s degree in neuroscience and behavior from Princeton University and his M.D. and Ph.D. degrees from Harvard Medical School. His graduate work led to the discovery of the causative gene in X-linked Duchenne and Becker muscular dystrophies. He continued in the field of genetics by conducting a postdoctoral fellowship at the Imperial Cancer Research Fund in London and moved on to lead the Neurogenetics group at Oxford. His research focused on the genetics behind neurodevelopmental problems in children such as autism, language impairment, and dyslexia. In fact, his group discovered FOXP2, the first gene specifically involved in human speech and language.
  At Oxford, President Monaco served as director of the Wellcome Trust Centre for Human Genetics, which seeks to discover genetic variants and how they affect disease. During his time as director, he helped double the size of the center. He then assumed the role of Pro-Vice Chancellor for Planning and Resources until taking on the role of president of Tufts in 2011.



Panel Discussion

How to get your first faculty position and what to look for in a start-up package


  Physician-scientists are considered an endangered species. Their existence continues to be threatened as they face unique pressures for producing clinically, starting a lab, acquiring funding, negotiating protected research time, and fighting misaligned institutional economic incentives. (Jain et al NEJM 2019). Training physician-scientists are most likley to succumb to these pressures at the resident, fellow, and junior faculty career transitions, and these times represent the leakiest part of the physician-scientist training pipeline.
  In this panel discussion for those planning on academic research careers, we invite you to hear from current mentors and employers of physician-scientists to learn what characteristics they look for in a potential junior faculty member, e.g. grants and publications. They will also discuss what institutional support their institutes provide for junior faculty transitioning into the independent portion of their careers.
  Target audience: residents/fellows/junior faculty; all training stages are welcome
